Habits We Can't Control

Listening · Part 3 Multiple matching FCE

1The task

You will hear five short extracts in which people talk about habits they find difficult to control.
For speakers 1 to 5, choose from the list (A–H) the habit each person has. Use each letter only once. There are three extra letters you do not need to use.
Aeating food that is bad for them
Bexercising far too much
Cbuying things they don't need
Dwatching too much television
Espending too many hours online
Fsleeping far too long
Gworking too hard
Hbeing late for everything

Why the wrong answers were tempting

In Part 3, the wrong options are designed to almost fit. Learn to spot the three traps, and the task becomes far easier.
Word matchThe speaker says the word, but it isn't their habit.
Almost fitsA detail that is true, but not the real habit.
Fact vs feelingWhat they do, versus how they feel about it.
Speaker 1C · buying things they don't need▼
The clue: he fills his basket and admits that whether he actually needs any of it is irrelevant. That single word settles it: the habit is the buying, not what he buys.
Trap A (food): he mentions a ready meal and junk food, so "eating badly" feels right. But the food ends up in the bin: he buys it, he doesn't eat it. Word match
Trap E (online): he is online the whole time. But being online is the means, not the habit. Ask yourself what he cannot stop doing. Almost fits
Speaker 2E · spending too many hours online▼
The clue: he never manages to log off until two in the morning. The habit is the hours online, whatever he is looking at.
Trap C (buying): he visits shopping sites, but says he rarely buys anything. The word "shopping" is there; the habit is not. Word match
Trap F (sleeping too long): he keeps falling asleep during the day, which sounds like oversleeping. But he sleeps too little: up at seven after going to bed at two. Almost fits
Trap A (food): he mentions overeating only to say his own habit is less harmful than that. He is comparing, not confessing. Fact vs feeling
Speaker 3B · exercising far too much▼
The clue: he doesn't feel right unless he is working on his fitness, and the long-term effects are starting to worry him. Note the attitude: concern, not denial.
Trap A (food): he talks about bigger meals and expensive food, but he always chooses food that is good for him. The food is a consequence of the exercise. Almost fits
Trap G (working too hard): "I always feel I have to be doing something" sounds like a workaholic. But what he must be doing is exercise, not work. Word match
Speaker 4D · watching too much television▼
The clue: the TV was on all the time at his parents' home and it is the same in his flat, and he is hooked on the same series. "Hooked on" is the giveaway.
Trap E (online): he mentions the internet, but to say that is what other people of his generation do, not him. Watch for "most people..." Fact vs feeling
Trap F (sleeping too long): he did struggle to get up before noon, but that was back in his student days. Now he gets up reasonably early. Past, not present. Almost fits
Speaker 5H · being late for everything▼
The clue: he is always the last to turn up, and something always holds him up. He even ends up running to get there.
Trap G (working too hard): the strongest trap in the whole task. He mentions a job to finish and an email to answer, so "working too hard" feels obvious. But those are the causes of the lateness, not the habit itself. Almost fits
Trap D (television): he mentions a TV programme that hasn't ended, another cause, not the habit. Word match
Trap F (sleeping too long): he actually tries to get up earlier. He is not oversleeping; the lack of sleep is a side effect. Fact vs feeling
The three letters left over: A, F and G. That is not an accident. Food, oversleeping and overwork are exactly the three ideas the speakers scatter through their answers as bait. In Part 3, the extra options are usually the most tempting surface details, not random ideas. If an option feels obvious after one sentence, be suspicious.

2The language of habit and tendency

This is how the speakers described habits that slip out of their control. Complete each sentence.
Use: keep · end up · can't help · tend to · somehow · rarely
I checking my phone, even in the middle of a conversation.
I only meant to look, but I always buying something I don't need.
I feeling that I should be doing something more useful.
I leave everything until the last minute.
I set off early, but I'm still the last to arrive.
